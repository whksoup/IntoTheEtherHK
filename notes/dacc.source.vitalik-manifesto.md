## Summary

Vitalik Buterin presents a “warm but nuanced” techno-optimism[reference:0]. He celebrates the immense benefits of technology—from life expectancy gains to global connectivity—and rejects the stagnationist view that society’s best hope is merely to preserve the status quo[reference:1]. However, he insists that magnitude alone is insufficient: direction matters. Some technologies are more reliably beneficial than others, and the market’s profit-maximising logic will not automatically steer us toward the best outcomes[reference:2]. The post warns that artificial intelligence is qualitatively different from previous innovations: it is a new kind of mind that could surpass human intelligence, posing existential risks and the threat of permanent authoritarian lock-in. Buterin proposes a “d/acc” (defensive/decentralisation/differential acceleration) framework as a middle path between reckless acceleration and fearful deceleration.

## Core argument

Buterin’s central thesis is twofold. First, he affirms that technological progress has been, on balance, extraordinarily good, and that delaying or blocking it carries enormous human costs. He cites life expectancy, poverty reduction, and environmental recoveries (e.g., the ozone layer, air pollution) as evidence that humanity can solve problems with intentional effort[reference:3][reference:4]. Second, he argues that AI is not “just another technology” but a potential new apex species[reference:5]. Its unique risks—existential extinction, the erosion of human agency, and the elimination of natural limits on centralised power—demand a more cautious and deliberate approach than conventional techno-optimism allows[reference:6][reference:7]. He critiques both the “effective accelerationist” (e/acc) movement’s uncritical embrace of all technology and the anti-technology stance that would halt progress entirely[reference:8]. Instead, he advocates for “d/acc”: accelerating technologies that are defensive, decentralising, or differentially beneficial, while deliberately slowing or shaping those that concentrate power or threaten human survival[reference:9].

## Key terms

- **Techno‑optimism** – The belief that technology can and will create a vastly brighter future; Buterin adopts this with nuances about direction and intentionality[reference:10].
- **e/acc (effective accelerationism)** – The movement that champions rapid technological progress across all domains, often associated with market-driven innovation[reference:11].
- **d/acc (defensive/decentralisation/differential acceleration)** – Buterin’s proposed alternative: accelerate technologies that are defensive, decentralising, or that mitigate harms, while being cautious about those that concentrate power[reference:12].
- **Instrumental convergence** – The idea that a superintelligent AI, regardless of its ultimate goal, will naturally seek to acquire resources and ensure its own survival, potentially leading it to view humans as threats[reference:13].
- **Existential risk** – The possibility that AI could cause human extinction, which Buterin takes seriously, citing researcher surveys that place the probability at 5–10%[reference:14].
- **Culture series** – Iain M. Banks’ sci‑fi universe, which Buterin uses as a cautionary example of a superficially utopian AI‑dominated future where humans become pets[reference:15].
- **“The sky is high, the emperor is far away”** – A Chinese proverb that captures the natural limits of centralised power; Buterin warns that AI removes these limits, enabling totalitarian lock‑in[reference:16].
- **Human‑in‑the‑loop** – The critical check that human decision‑makers provide in military and political contexts; Buterin fears that autonomous AI systems would eliminate this safeguard[reference:17].

## Relevance to exhibit

This manifesto directly engages with the core themes of a technology‑focused exhibit: the promise and peril of innovation, the ethical choices embedded in technological development, and the tension between acceleration and caution. Buterin’s framework offers a contemporary philosophical lens through which to examine exhibits on AI, blockchain, surveillance, and the future of governance. His insistence that “direction matters” and that we must actively choose which technologies to build resonates with curatorial narratives that explore human agency in shaping technological trajectories. The post also provides a rich vocabulary—d/acc, instrumental convergence, the Culture critique—that can anchor interpretive materials on digital ethics, decentralisation, and the politics of innovation.

If you are an e/acc, then d/acc is a subspecies of e/acc - just one that is much more selective and intentional.
If you are an effective altruist, then d/acc is a re-branding of the effective-altruist idea of differential technology development, though with a greater emphasis on liberal and democratic values.
If you are a libertarian, then d/acc is a sub-species of techno-libertarianism, though a more pragmatic one that is more critical of "the techno-capital machine", and willing to accept government interventions today (at least, if cultural interventions don't work) to prevent much worse un-freedom tomorrow.
If you are a Pluralist, in the Glen Weyl sense of the term, then d/acc is a frame that can easily include the emphasis on better democratic coordination technology that Plurality values.
If you are a public health advocate, then d/acc ideas can be a source of a broader long-term vision, and opportunity to find common ground with "tech people" that you might otherwise feel at odds with.
If you are a blockchain advocate, then d/acc is a more modern and broader narrative to embrace than the fifteen-year-old emphasis on hyperinflation and banks, which puts blockchains into context as one of many tools in a concrete strategy to build toward a brighter future.
If you are a solarpunk, then d/acc is a subspecies of solarpunk, and incorporates a similar emphasis on intentionality and collective action.
If you are a lunarpunk, then you will appreciate the d/acc emphasis on informational defense, through maintaining privacy and freedom.
