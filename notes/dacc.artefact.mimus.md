## Description

_Mimus_ is an interactive robotic installation created by Madeline Gannon in 2017 at the Frank-Ratchye STUDIO for Creative Inquiry. Rather than functioning as a conventional industrial robot that executes predetermined tasks, Mimus behaves as an autonomous mechanical creature whose movements emerge from continuous perception of the people around it. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Built on an ABB IRB 6700 industrial robot, Mimus combines an array of overhead depth sensors, real-time perception software, behavioral algorithms, and robot control systems to produce fluid, lifelike movement. Instead of following scripted choreography, the robot continually evaluates visitors according to characteristics such as proximity, activity, and engagement, selecting the "most interesting" person to observe or follow. If a visitor remains inactive for too long, Mimus becomes "bored" and seeks another interaction. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

The installation intentionally frames the robot not as an industrial tool but as an animal inhabiting an enclosure. Its movement vocabulary was designed to evoke recognizable emotional states—curiosity, caution, excitement, intimidation—allowing visitors to intuitively understand the machine's intentions through body language rather than explicit interfaces. This transforms communication between human and machine into a fundamentally embodied experience. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

As an artefact, Mimus represents a shift in robotics from command-and-control toward legible autonomy. Rather than maximizing efficiency or replacing human labor, it investigates how increasingly autonomous machines might coexist with people through transparency, predictability, and mutual understanding.

Human–Machine Coexistence

## Curatorial notes

Mimus exemplifies a form of techno-optimism that prioritizes communication over control. Rather than asking how autonomous systems can become more capable, it asks how they can become more understandable.

This distinction is increasingly important as AI systems acquire greater autonomy. Technical capability alone does not produce trust; legibility does. By giving an industrial robot expressive body language and intelligible behavioural patterns, Mimus demonstrates that humans naturally negotiate relationships through movement, posture, and intention long before language enters the picture.

Viewed through the lens of defensive technology, Mimus accelerates an often-overlooked capability: reducing the probability of misunderstanding between humans and autonomous machines. As robots move beyond factory cages into public spaces, homes, hospitals, and infrastructure, the ability for humans to accurately infer a machine's intentions becomes a safety technology in its own right.

The work also challenges the dominant narrative that automation necessarily implies displacement. Instead, it proposes coexistence as a design problem. The future is not simply one in which robots become more intelligent, but one in which humans and machines develop shared behavioural conventions that make collaboration intuitive and trustworthy.

Mimus therefore represents a broader vision of technological progress: intelligence that is interpretable, autonomy that remains socially legible, and robotics designed to cultivate empathy rather than fear.
