# Openwater

## Description

Openwater is a medical technology company founded by [!WARNING]  that aims to dramatically reduce the cost and increase the accessibility of advanced diagnostic and therapeutic medical devices by leveraging semiconductor manufacturing, computational imaging, photonics, ultrasound, and open-source hardware/software.

Its core thesis is that many capabilities traditionally associated with expensive hospital infrastructure (MRI, ultrasound imaging, neuromodulation, targeted therapies) can be rebuilt as software-defined, wearable devices manufactured using consumer electronics supply chains. Rather than producing single-purpose medical devices, Openwater proposes a modular "pan-disease" platform consisting primarily of:

- **Open-Motion** — optical imaging using coherent near-infrared light to measure blood flow, blood volume and tissue micromotion.
- **Open-LIFU** — low-intensity focused ultrasound (LIFU) for non-invasive therapeutic intervention and neuromodulation.

The company explicitly frames this vision as the **"Silicon Hospital"**: replacing bespoke medical hardware with programmable, software-driven platforms whose capabilities improve through shared data, open development and AI. Rather than proprietary hardware ecosystems, Openwater has committed to open-source hardware, firmware, software, clinical datasets and safety documentation under AGPL licensing. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

From a d/acc perspective, Openwater represents one of the clearest examples of **defensive accelerationism applied to healthcare**: using open technological progress to reduce the costs of medicine while avoiding centralized control over life-saving infrastructure.

## Section

### Why it matters

Openwater attempts to do for medical devices what commodity semiconductor manufacturing did for computing.

Instead of treating MRI scanners, stroke detectors or therapeutic ultrasound systems as distinct, highly specialized machines, it argues that they are ultimately combinations of:

- optics
- acoustics
- sensors
- computation
- machine learning
- manufacturing

Once viewed through this lens, they become software-defined hardware platforms whose costs should asymptotically approach those of smartphones rather than million-dollar hospital equipment.

This is an unusually software-native interpretation of medicine.

---

### Platform thinking instead of device thinking

A notable feature is that Openwater is not organized around diseases.

Instead it proposes a general-purpose platform:

```
Physical modalities
↓
light
ultrasound
electromagnetics

↓

Programmable hardware

↓

Software

↓

Disease-specific protocols

↓

Cancer
Stroke
Depression
Parkinson's
Autoimmune disease
Brain-computer interfaces
etc.
```

This resembles the evolution of general-purpose computing far more than conventional medical-device development.

---

### Open source as infrastructure

Perhaps Openwater's most distinctive feature is its commitment to making nearly everything public:

- hardware designs
- firmware
- software
- AI tooling
- documentation
- safety work
- clinical data (where feasible)

The rationale is not ideological alone.

Medical device development suffers from enormous duplicated engineering effort because every company rebuilds similar validation, tooling and infrastructure from scratch.

Openwater argues that shared engineering allows innovation to occur at the application layer instead.

This mirrors:

- Linux
- ROS in robotics
- CERN open science
- open semiconductor tooling
- open AI models

rather than traditional medtech.

### Semiconductor economics applied to healthcare

A recurring theme is exploiting manufacturing curves.

Historically:

- televisions
- smartphones
- cameras
- GPUs

all became dramatically cheaper because semiconductor manufacturing scales extraordinarily well.

Openwater argues that medical imaging and therapeutic devices have largely failed to benefit from these curves because they are manufactured as bespoke medical equipment rather than consumer electronics.

Its ambition is therefore not merely technological improvement but cost collapse.

This abundance-oriented framing strongly overlaps with d/acc thinking.

---

### Relationship to AI

Unlike many AI-first healthcare companies, Openwater positions AI as a secondary layer.

Their stack is approximately:

```
Physics

↓

Sensors

↓

Hardware platform

↓

Signal processing

↓

AI

↓

Clinical interpretation
```

AI becomes valuable because inexpensive hardware enables orders of magnitude more physiological measurements than today's healthcare systems can collect.

The company frequently describes every deployed device as another contributor to a shared physiological data network.

### Clinical direction

Current publicly described areas include:

- stroke detection
- treatment-resistant depression
- glioblastoma
- blood-flow imaging
- neurological disorders
- cardiovascular monitoring
- brain-computer interfaces
- veterinary medicine

Most therapeutic systems remain in clinical research rather than routine clinical deployment.

The company is careful to distinguish current research devices from future commercial ambitions.

### Relationship to Sanctuary Technologies

Openwater aligns closely with the Sanctuary Technologies worldview because it attempts to decentralize one of civilization's most concentrated forms of infrastructure: advanced medicine.

Traditional healthcare depends upon scarce institutions:

- hospitals
- MRI suites
- specialists
- centralized diagnostic equipment

Openwater instead imagines:

- portable diagnostics
- programmable therapy
- open manufacturing
- local deployment
- globally shared software

The result is a healthcare stack that becomes progressively more resilient against institutional bottlenecks.

This is analogous to:

- local-first software
- distributed manufacturing
- open-source AI
- mesh communications

but applied to healthcare.

## Curatorial notes

Openwater occupies an unusual intersection between:

- open-source software culture
- semiconductor economics
- biotechnology
- neurotechnology
- medical devices
- effective acceleration

It is one of the strongest examples of **d/acc principles escaping software** and becoming embodied in physical infrastructure.

The project also reflects a broader transition from **closed industrial medical equipment** toward **programmable, networked medical platforms**.

Unlike many biotech startups whose moat lies in patents, Openwater attempts to build its moat around manufacturing quality, execution, community participation and data network effects.

This makes it a particularly interesting artefact for studying post-proprietary innovation ecosystems.

## Ideas

- Openwater suggests a future where hospitals become increasingly software-defined rather than building-defined.
- Medicine may undergo the same transition computing experienced between 1970–2005: from scarce institutional machines to ubiquitous personal devices.
- Open-source clinical infrastructure may become more strategically important than proprietary algorithms.
- Sanctuary technologies need not only defend against external threats—they can also defend against scarcity itself.
- The "Silicon Hospital" is a compelling conceptual bridge between d/acc, open hardware and abundance economics.
- Openwater demonstrates that open-source can function as a scaling strategy rather than merely a licensing philosophy.
- The project invites comparison with Linux, RepRap, OpenBCI, CERN and other commons-based technological ecosystems.
- Worth exploring alongside Vitalik Buterin's writing on d/acc, especially around decentralizing critical infrastructure and reducing civilizational fragility.
