## The question:
How closely is the sanctuary technologies movement entangled with cryptocurrency? Why is the future of open source technology so deeply interrelated with ethereum, and isn't the fact that Vitalik is pushing for his own currency as the common 'decentralized currency' going against the idea of decentralization?
## Definition
But notice that the examples Buterin reaches for to define the category are mostly not crypto at all — Starlink, locally-running open-weight LLMs, Signal, and Community Notes. His stated test for whether something qualifies is functional: does it stay robust under outside pressure? By that definition, Ethereum is one component of a broader stack, not the whole thing. So the concept is broader than crypto; the movement carrying the banner is Ethereum-centric. Keeping those two apart is important, because a lot of the critique you're raising applies to the second, not the first.

Open source vastly predates crypto and dwarfs it (Linux, the entire web stack, Signal, most of what you use daily). Nothing about open source needs a blockchain. So when Ethereum people talk as if they're the vanguard of open-source freedom, part of that is movement branding.
That said, there's one genuinely substantive argument underneath the branding, and it's worth stating in its strongest form. Open source has a chronic weakness: it's brilliant at producing code but bad at (a) funding public goods sustainably and (b) making credible, un-revocable commitments. Maintainers burn out; a company that open-sources something can still change the rules, wall off the API, or get acquired. The Ethereum pitch is that a decentralized ledger lets you encode rules in mathematics rather than policy — a protocol that keeps running even if its makers walk away, and funding mechanisms (quadratic funding, Gitcoin-style rounds) that pay for public goods without a corporate owner. Whether Ethereum actually delivers that is contestable, but that's the real claim, not "open source runs on Ethereum."

# **Now the core question: isn't Buterin promoting his own currency as the decentralized substrate, which both contradicts decentralization and enriches him?**
Prong one is the self-enrichment charge: he shills ETH because he holds ETH. Here the empirical facts genuinely cut against the critique. His known holdings are roughly 224,000–240,000 ETH, about 0.20% of supply, down from 0.91% in 2015, and he stated in 2018 he'd never held more than ~0.9% — a claim on-chain analysts have verified. He's given away staggering amounts: he burned ~410 trillion SHIB (worth around $6 billion at the time) and sent the rest to India's COVID relief fund, donated to Ukraine, funded privacy messengers like Session and SimpleX, and in January 2026 donated 16,384 ETH (~$43M) to open-source infrastructure. He says he hasn't sold ETH for personal gain since 2018, and because nearly all his wealth is on-chain and publicly traceable, that's unusually checkable. So "selfishly benefits" is the weakest version of the attack: a genuinely selfish founder does not give away his single largest windfall and shrink his own share.

Prong two is subtler and much harder for him to escape: the structural conflict. Even a saintly Buterin still holds ~0.2% of an asset that could be worth hundreds of billions, so his advocacy and his net worth still point the same direction — you don't have to allege bad faith for the incentive to exist. And promoting Ethereum as the credibly neutral base layer is itself a competitive, arguably centralizing move: there are other chains, and anointing one substrate as the principled choice advantages it over rivals. "Neutrality" then does double duty as a moral principle and a marketing moat, and those two can't be cleanly separated. There's also a person-dependency problem visible in the sources themselves: the entire ecosystem still reorganizes around his blog posts and tweets, which is itself a form of centralization he hasn't dissolved.

## What makes us trust him?
His answer to the structural charge isn't "trust me," it's "measure it, and hold me to building myself out of power." The concrete pieces:
Credible neutrality as the actual product. His claim is that Ethereum's entire value is that no one — including him — can control it, so anything that let him extract rents or dictate outcomes would destroy the thing he's promoting. He's argued that if alignment comes to mean "having the right friends," the concept has failed.
The walkaway test. He's made this his headline benchmark: the network should keep running safely even if he and the core developers disappear entirely. He pairs it with an "insider attack test" — how much damage could the team do if it turned malicious? A system optimized to pass those is, by design, one where the founder's ongoing importance trends toward zero.
Structurally reducing his own role. The EF mandate positions the Foundation as a neutral steward that explicitly does not build products or direct the ecosystem, and he's been pushing a "DAO renaissance" so the network's future depends on durable institutions rather than, in his words, charismatic leaders. His declining token share fits the same pattern.
Pluralism. Because sanctuary tech explicitly includes Starlink, Signal, and local LLMs, he can say he's not claiming everything must be Ethereum — just that Ethereum should be one honest tool among several.
