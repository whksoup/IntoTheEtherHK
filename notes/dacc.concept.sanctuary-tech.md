Let me search specifically for that.Let me check specifically for the "misuse" angle — whether the reframing was a response to d/acc being co-opted.The two terms come from the same person and the same worldview, but they operate at different levels of abstraction — which is really what the "misuse" story is about.

**d/acc** is the broad philosophy. Vitalik Buterin coined it in his November 2023 "My techno-optimism" essay as a deliberate counter to e/acc (effective accelerationism). The "d" is intentionally overloaded — it stands for defense, democracy, decentralization, or differential technological development. The core claim is that you shouldn't accelerate all technology indiscriminately; you should accelerate but prioritize advances that improve defense over harm and distribute power rather than concentrate it. It builds explicitly on Nick Bostrom's earlier idea of differential technological development, and it's domain-agnostic — it covers biosecurity, cybersecurity, resilient physical infrastructure, and a healthy information ecosystem all at once. It's a lens for how humanity as a whole should steer technology.

**Sanctuary technologies** is much newer and much narrower. Buterin introduced it in a manifesto on X on March 3, 2026, and the Ethereum Foundation then formalized it ten days later in an official mandate document. He defined it as free open-source technologies that let people live, work, talk to each other, manage risk and build wealth, and collaborate on shared goals, in a way that optimizes for robustness to outside pressures. The goal he attached to it is "de-totalization" — limiting the likelihood that any government, corporation, or dominant actor achieves total control, while ensuring opponents are not entirely shut out, or as he put it, creating digital islands of stability in a chaotic era. Crucially, the test is functional: robustness to outside pressure. His examples span beyond crypto — Starlink, locally-running open-weight LLMs, Signal, and Community Notes — with Ethereum positioned as one component of that stack.

**On the "misuse" framing you mentioned:** there's a real dynamic here, though I'd be careful about how it's usually told. d/acc did get diluted after 2023. It spawned branded spin-offs — Entrepreneur First launched a "def/acc" accelerator program, and the term got absorbed into VC-style pitches about "growth" and "human agency" that stripped out the anti-concentration-of-power core. When a term becomes a fundable buzzword, it stops discriminating between things.

What I'd push back on slightly: I didn't find Buterin explicitly saying "I'm abandoning d/acc because people misused it." The more defensible reading is that sanctuary tech is a *sharpening* rather than a replacement — it takes the defensive/decentralization strand of d/acc and gives it (a) a concrete mission owner (the Ethereum ecosystem) and (b) a single testable criterion (does it hold up under outside pressure?) that's harder to co-opt than a four-way acronym. The EF mandate even attaches a checklist, CROPS — Censorship Resistance; Open Source and Free, as in Freedom; Privacy; and Security. That specificity is precisely what a diluted term lacks, so the "misuse led to the reframe" story is plausible as *motivation* even if it's more interpretation than stated fact.

So the cleanest way to hold them: d/acc is the general theory of which technologies civilization should race to build; sanctuary tech is a concrete, testable subset — the "escape hatch" tools — that Buterin is now asking one specific community to actually ship.

One caveat worth flagging: a lot of the sanctuary-tech coverage comes from crypto-market outlets that interleave it with ETH price commentary, so the framing gets colored by people talking their book. Buterin's own X manifesto and the EF mandate are the primary sources if you want the unfiltered version.
